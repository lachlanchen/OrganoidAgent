# Immune Cell Morphodynamics and Analysis in Organoid Interactions

## Biological Significance of Immune Cell Shape Changes During Infiltration

Immune cells often undergo dramatic shape changes - such as elongation and front-back polarization - when migrating through tissues or infiltrating organoids. These morphodynamic changes are not just incidental; they have important biological implications. For example, macrophages polarizing to a pro-healing (M2) phenotype adopt an elongated, spindle-like shape, whereas pro-inflammatory (M1) macrophages become flattened and round. Experimentally forcing macrophages to elongate (via micropatterned surfaces) was shown to induce M2 gene expression even without cytokine signals, underscoring that cell shape itself can direct immune cell function. Thus, an elongated morphology in macrophages correlates with a healing, anti-inflammatory state, linking physical form to cellular phenotype.

For motile immune cells like T cells and dendritic cells, shape changes are crucial to navigate complex 3D environments. Amoeboid migration is a common strategy: cells become polarized with a leading edge and squeeze their bodies through pre-existing pores in the matrix. High-impact studies have revealed that leukocytes actively probe their surroundings and use their nucleus as a "gauge" - by pushing the nucleus forward - to find paths of least resistance through dense tissue. This nucleus-forward configuration helps them select larger pores over smaller ones, ensuring faster transit with minimal matrix remodeling. In confined spaces where even the largest gaps are too small, immune cells can adapt by assembling a contractile actin pool in the cell center that pushes outward to widen a path for the nucleus. This transient "path-clearing" mechanism, recently demonstrated in leukocytes moving through extremely dense environments, highlights how cytoskeletal reorganization and shape changes enable continued migration. Notably, disrupting that central actin pool causes an imbalance - the leading edge over-extends and the cell body gets stuck - impairing migration in tight spaces. Such findings illustrate that morphological polarization and deformation are integral to efficient immune cell infiltration.

Within organoid models, similar observations have been made. A 2024 study described human "immuno-organoids" in which tissue-resident memory T cells (T_RM) naturally integrate into intestinal organoids and patrol the epithelium. Live imaging showed these T cells dynamically crawling and even inserting themselves between epithelial cells. In fact, the authors captured T cells adopting an "elongated flossing" shape to wedge between basal and lateral surfaces of organoid epithelial cells. This elongated, thread-like morphology appears to facilitate their surveillance of the barrier, essentially mimicking tissue infiltration behavior. Such examples reinforce that immune cells adjust their shape (elongating, polarizing, and squeezing through gaps) as a functional adaptation - whether to promote a particular activation state or to physically navigate complex microenvironments. In summary, cell shape is tightly coupled to immune cell function and migration, and high-impact studies in Nature and Science have established that these shape changes are meaningful indicators of immune cell role and efficiency during infiltration.

## Computational Methods for Analyzing Brightfield Time-Lapse Interactions

Studying immune cell-organoid interactions often involves long-term live imaging. Brightfield or phase-contrast microscopy is attractive for such time-lapse experiments because it avoids fluorescent labels (reducing perturbation and phototoxicity) and can capture cells continuously. However, analyzing label-free brightfield videos poses significant challenges: immune cells and organoid structures produce complex grayscale images with low contrast and overlapping textures. Recent advances show that sophisticated computational methods - particularly those leveraging machine learning - are increasingly capable of extracting quantitative data from brightfield time-lapse imagery, and prioritizing these methods can strengthen a study's impact.

Several notable tools have emerged. OrganoID is a deep learning platform (published in PLoS Comput Bio, 2022) that enables high-throughput analysis of organoid images. It uses convolutional neural networks to automatically segment and track individual organoids in brightfield or phase-contrast movies, even when organoids cluster or change shape. In one demonstration, OrganoID accurately tracked pancreatic tumor organoids over days of imaging, achieving >95% agreement with manual measurements of organoid count and size. Such automated analysis of organoid growth and morphology over time would be highly suitable in an immune-organoid co-culture setting, where one could quantitatively link immune cell infiltration to organoid size or shape changes.

Another state-of-the-art example is LabelFreeTracker, a machine learning method introduced in 2025 for label-free analysis of cells in 3D organoids. This approach uses a trained U-Net network to transform brightfield images into predicted nuclear and membrane fluorescence signals, essentially in silico labeling each cell. By doing so, it becomes possible to segment and track individual cells inside organoids over time without any actual fluorescent reporters. Kok et al. report that LabelFreeTracker can perform single-cell tracking, shape and volume measurements, and lineage tracing in intestinal organoids from just transmitted-light images. The ability to quantify immune cell migration and morphology inside an organoid in brightfield (freeing up fluorescence channels for other reporters) is a major technological step. Emphasizing such computational innovation - for example, using AI to discern immune cell positions and shapes in label-free videos - would make the story more compelling for a broad journal like Nature, which values methodological novelty.

When immune cells are co-cultured with organoids, distinguishing them in images is critical. Traditional image analysis often fails in these complex co-cultures, but new solutions are emerging. A 2023 study developed a machine learning-empowered image cytometry pipeline for immune-organoid co-cultures. By integrating automated microscopy with grayscale image processing (contrast enhancement, edge detection) and a trained classifier, the authors could reliably detect organoids in the presence of dense immune cell clusters. This allowed high-throughput quantification of organoid number, size, and shape even when immune cells were swirling around or adhering to the organoids. In essence, they created an "Organoid App" to separate and analyze organoids within co-culture images, solving a key technical hurdle. Likewise, other groups have started sharing curated datasets and pretrained models to spur progress in this area. For instance, a new public dataset of immune-enriched pancreatic cancer organoids (phase-contrast images with immune cell co-culture) was released in 2024, alongside algorithms for object detection and segmentation. This dataset - OrganoIDNetData, with over 33,000 organoids annotated - is meant to serve as a benchmark for developing better organoid and immune-cell image analysis methods. By leveraging such computational tools and datasets, one can analyze dynamic interactions (like immune cell infiltration rates, organoid structural changes, cell shape metrics) quantitatively from brightfield time-lapse videos. Highlighting this computational angle in your story - for example, using deep learning to track immune cell morphodynamics in organoids - would align well with the data-rich, technology-driven narratives favored by high-impact journals.

Another way to elevate the project is to place it in context of emerging automated or "agentic" imaging systems. Increasingly, labs are employing smart microscopes and integrated platforms that can perform experiments with minimal human intervention - something that high-impact journals often highlight as cutting-edge. Traditional manual handling and imaging of organoids (or immune cell co-cultures) can be labor-intensive and variable. In line with this, several systems and case studies show how semi-automated approaches are already advancing organoid and immune cell imaging:

- Robotic Organoid Culture and AI Monitoring: A recent example is the CellXpress.ai system, an integrated automated cell culture platform for organoids. It combines robotic liquid handling, an incubator, and a built-in microscope with AI-driven image analysis. At Emory University, researchers installed this system to maintain human brain organoids for weeks and continuously analyze them. The system can culture up to 20,000 organoids in parallel and uses artificial intelligence to monitor cell morphology and differentiation status in real-time. Essentially, the microscope's software "agent" evaluates whether organoid cells have matured or not by recognizing patterns in brightfield images. This level of automation - tending the cultures and making observations automatically - vastly improves throughput and consistency, illustrating the concept of an agentic imaging pipeline. In your story, referencing such technology shows that you are at the forefront of using AI to drive experiments (not just analyze them after the fact).
- Intelligent Imaging for Immune Cell Dynamics: In the realm of immune cell imaging, researchers have also developed automated tracking and analysis frameworks. One notable case is UFMTrack (Under-Flow Migration Tracker), a framework designed to study the full cascade of immune cell extravasation in vitro under shear flow. This was applied to time-lapse microscopy of immune cells crawling and transmigrating across an endothelial monolayer in a microfluidic device. The UFMTrack pipeline uses machine learning for segmentation and event detection, enabling automated, scalable quantification of each step of the immune cell's journey (rolling, firm arrest, diapedesis, etc.) without manual annotation. A commentary in Cell highlighted that "AI-enabled autonomous tracking pipelines such as UFMTrack allow robust quantification of multistep interactions" during immune cell extravasation. This is a prime example of an agentic imaging system: the microscope and software together identify rare events (e.g. a T cell squeezing between endothelial cells) and measure them, essentially automating an experiment that previously needed constant human observation. If your work involves immune cells infiltrating organoids, one could draw parallels to extravasation and note that similar semi-automated approaches (with computer vision "agents" detecting when a cell enters an organoid, for instance) could be employed.
- High-Throughput Imaging and Analysis Pipelines: Even without fully robotic systems, many studies have built semi-automated image analysis workflows to handle complex co-cultures. We already discussed the organoid co-culture image analysis pipeline (a form of automation in analysis). Another example is the use of existing software like Ilastik or TrackMate with custom machine learning models to automatically track immune cells in videos. One recent pipeline combined a StarDist neural network for cell detection with Fiji's TrackMate for linking positions, and successfully tracked cancer cells and T cells in both fluorescent and brightfield microscopy data. The key point is that the community is moving toward smart microscopy - using algorithms to guide both image acquisition and analysis. Emphasizing that your approach includes, say, a machine learning model that flags interesting immune cell behaviors in organoid movies (and perhaps triggers higher-resolution imaging of those events) could make the story stand out.
